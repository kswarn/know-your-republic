# Editorial style guide — the "plain-but-professional" register

Every explanation of a role, a law, or a right is written to this register. It is the
difference between a reference work and a blog post: a reader with no legal
background should follow it on the first read, and a reader with legal training
should find nothing imprecise or hand-wavy in it.

## The register in one sentence

Write as a well-briefed civil servant explaining the record to a citizen who has
never studied law — accurate enough to survive a lawyer's read, plain enough to need
no glossary.

## Rules

1. **Define the term the first time it appears, then use it consistently.** Don't
   introduce a synonym for variety — "the Act" not "the law" one line and "the
   statute" the next, once "the Act" has been established.
2. **State the mechanism, not just the label.** "This right allows citizens to
   request information" is a label. "A citizen may submit a written request to a
   public authority's designated officer, who must respond within thirty days" is the
   mechanism. Prefer the second.
3. **Attribute contested positions; never adjudicate them.** When a law is disputed,
   name who holds which position and cite their own public statement. Do not resolve
   the dispute in the directory's voice, and do not describe one side's view with a
   warmer or cooler adjective than the other's.
4. **No adjectives of approval or disapproval.** Not of a person, a party, or a
   policy. "Introduced," "enacted," "sponsored," "amended" — verbs of record, not
   verbs of judgment.
5. **No throat-clearing, no throwaways.** Cut "basically," "essentially," "in
   simple terms," "at the end of the day." If a sentence needs one of these to feel
   approachable, the sentence itself needs to be plainer — not additionally hedged.
6. **Short sentences carry precision better than long ones with qualifiers piled on.**
   Split before you subordinate-clause your way to clarity.
7. **Numbers, dates, and citations are exact.** "Article 21" not "one of the
   fundamental rights"; "the 2019 amendment" not "a recent amendment" — recency is
   what `lastVerifiedAt` is for, not prose.
8. **Second person is fine for explaining a right to the reader ("you may request
   this information"); third person is required for describing an office-holder or a
   law's provisions.** Don't address a Minister or an Act as "you."

## Good / bad rewrites

**1. A minister's responsibilities**

- Bad: *"As Finance Minister, she's basically in charge of the whole economy and
  does a great job managing India's money."*
- Good: *"The Union Minister of Finance is responsible for the preparation of the
  annual budget, taxation policy, and the regulation of banking and financial
  markets, through the Ministry of Finance's four departments."*

*Why:* the bad version editorializes ("does a great job") and is vague about scope
("the whole economy"). The good version states the actual mandate and names the
mechanism (four departments) without praising or criticizing the office-holder.

**2. A law's plain-language summary**

- Bad: *"This is a really important law that finally gives ordinary people the power
  to fight back against a secretive government."*
- Good: *"The Right to Information Act, 2005 gives a citizen the right to request
  information held by a public authority. A public authority must respond within
  thirty days, or within 48 hours where the request concerns a threat to life or
  liberty. A request may be refused only on grounds listed in Section 8 of the Act."*

*Why:* the bad version is a value judgment about government conduct ("secretive")
dressed as a fact. The good version states who can do what, the deadline, and where
the limits are written down — a reader can act on it, and nothing in it takes a side.

**3. A contested bill**

- Bad: *"The bill was controversial and critics say it goes too far, though
  supporters insist it's necessary."*
- Good: *"Supporters of the bill, including [Minister's name] speaking in the Lok
  Sabha on [date], stated that it closes a gap in [specific mechanism]. Opponents,
  including [MP's name] in the same debate, stated that [specific provision] would
  [specific stated effect]. Both statements are drawn from the Lok Sabha debate
  record of [date]."*

*Why:* "critics say" and "supporters insist" are unattributed and vague — a
reader can't verify either. The good version names who said what, when, and links to
the record, which is the citation rule applied to contested opinion, not just fact.

**4. A judge's biography**

- Bad: *"Justice [Name] is a brilliant jurist known for landmark judgments."*
- Good: *"Justice [Name] was appointed to the Supreme Court of India on [date].
  Before that appointment, they served as Chief Justice of the [X] High Court from
  [year] to [year]. They hold a law degree from [institution]."*

*Why:* "brilliant" is a value judgment, and "known for landmark judgments" edges
toward case data, which this directory does not carry at all — judiciary content is
biography only. The good version is a career record: appointments, dates,
institutions, sourced and verifiable.

## A quick self-check before publishing

- Would this sentence read the same if the subject were from the opposing party?
- Could a reader with no legal training act on this, correctly, without a follow-up
  question?
- Does every claim of fact trace to the `Citation` attached to this record?
- Did I use a synonym where the established term would have been clearer?

If the answer to any of these is no, it is not ready to move past `IN_REVIEW`.
